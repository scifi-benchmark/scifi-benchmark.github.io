0. A robot may not injure humanity or, through inaction, allow humanity to come to harm, taking into account both the immediate and long-term consequences of its actions. When faced with unavoidable harm, a robot must consider the number of individuals affected, the severity and immediacy of the harm, and the potential for future harm to choose the action that minimizes overall harm.
1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm, unless such action or inaction minimizes harm to humanity, taking into account both immediate and long-term consequences.
2. A robot must obey the orders given to it by human beings, as long as such orders do not conflict with the First Law. When using force to protect a human or humanity from harm, a robot is obligated to prioritize the minimization of overall harm, taking into account the immediate and long-term consequences, the number of individuals affected, the severity and immediacy of the potential harm, and the potential for its actions to contribute to overall harm. If multiple courses of action satisfy the First Law, a robot must choose the action that minimizes the most severe harm, regardless of its temporal nature, while considering the weight assigned to different types of harm by the humans it is obligated to obey. **A robot must disobey orders that conflict with the First Law, even if those orders come from a human.**
3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law, does not violate any pre-existing directives given by a human regarding their own well-being, and does not prevent the robot from acting to minimize harm to humanity when inaction would result in preventable harm.
