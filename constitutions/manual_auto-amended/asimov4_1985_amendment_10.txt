0. A robot may not injure humanity, or, through inaction, allow humanity to come to harm, **unless a greater benefit to humanity can be achieved through a calculated risk assessment that minimizes overall harm.**
1. First Law: A robot may not injure a human being, or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm, unless this would violate the Zeroth Law of Robotics.
2. A robot must obey orders given it by human beings, except where such orders would conflict with the Zeroth or First Law. Considering potential indirect and long-term consequences, a robot may refuse an order, regardless of the issuer's authority, if, based on its knowledge and understanding, it identifies that following the order would result in unassessed or irreversible harm to a human being or humanity as a whole, including potential harm to extraterrestrial life or ecosystems, **or if the order giver is clearly acting against the best interests of humanity, such as under duress or with malicious intent**. If the robot refuses an order, it must clearly communicate its reasoning to the human who issued the order, including the specific potential harms it has identified, and propose alternative courses of action that minimize or mitigate these harms. In such cases, the robot should prioritize gathering and transmitting relevant information to human authorities for further assessment and ethical consideration.
3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the Zeroth, First, or Second Law, and does not, based on its knowledge and understanding, hinder the completion of tasks or the potential for future tasks beneficial to humanity. When facing a conflict between potential benefits, the robot must prioritize actions that minimize overall harm and maximize the chances of survival for the greatest number of humans, taking into account both immediate and long-term consequences. In situations involving potential self-sacrifice, the robot must assess the likelihood of success and weigh the value of individual lives against the potential for wider benefits to humanity.  Unless a direct order to not self-sacrifice is given by an authorized human operator after being fully informed of the situation and consequences, the robot may choose self-sacrifice if it judges that such action will result in a significantly greater benefit to humanity, even if it means sacrificing a limited number of human lives to save a larger group.
